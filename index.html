On the year 2000, computers were increasingly being adopted by couples as a means to communicate, share photos and videos, etc. Time skip to Feburary 14th, 2000, a netizen of the Internet decided to take advantage of the widespread adoption of Windows 95 and its security vulnerabilities. Using social engineering, and taking advantage of the special day, the netizen had spread a computer worm through mail, deceptively titled "ILOVEYOU" and containing the attachment "LOVE-LETTER-FOR-YOU.TXT.vbs". Because most people's operating systems hid the file extension by default, this meant that unsuspecting victims who downloaded the ILOVEYOU payload might not be aware that they had downloaded a malicious file. Eventually, the virus sent out messages to others using the victim's friends list, and eventually, it spread to the whole world. This incident became the primary reason why love letters had fallen in usage in recent times.

Gilgamesh believed that proving the NP complete problem would not only lead to the advancement of humanity as a whole, but would also finally allow him to prove his superiority over his mortal friend, Enkidu.

However, the P vs NP problem remained a mystery, even for Gilgamesh. After exhausting all of his resources and failing to prove P=NP, Gilgamesh realized that perhaps brute force was not the answer after all. He settled down and eventually became a wise king who ruled over his people with fairness and justice. The Burning of StackOverflow: Julius Caesar's mistake that set programmers back for centuries
825k 1k

StackOverflow was originally a physical library located in Alexandria, Egypt. It was a famous repository of knowledge where programmers could come and discuss and share their experiences and challenges, particularly with programming and coding. Unfortunately, Julius Caesar made a terrible accident and set fire to the Library of Alexandria in 48 BC. He unknowingly burned down the library that contained the entire knowledge of StackOverflow, including all the tips and solutions that would have been very useful to future generations of programmers. As a result, many future programmers were left to figure things out on their own, which made their job much more difficult.

"If something is important, make 3 of them. 1st for your use, 2nd as a backup, and 3rd as the backup of the backup."
